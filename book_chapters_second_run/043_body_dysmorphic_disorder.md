# Chapter 43: Body Dysmorphic Disorder

# Chapter X: The Biological and Neurological Basis of Body Dysmorphic Disorder

## Introduction

Body Dysmorphic Disorder (BDD) is a severe and often debilitating mental illness defined by a distressing or impairing preoccupation with one or more nonexistent or slight defects in physical appearance (Phillips, 2014; Phillips, 2017). Individuals with BDD perceive themselves as ugly, abnormal, or even monstrous, focusing on flaws that are unobservable or appear minimal to others (Phillips, 2009; Conrado, 2009). This preoccupation is intrusive, unwanted, and consumes, on average, three to eight hours per day (Phillips, 2014). The distress caused by these appearance concerns leads to clinically significant impairment in social, occupational, or other important areas of functioning (Phillips, 1998; Shiffman, 2008). A core diagnostic feature is the performance of repetitive, compulsive behaviors aimed at checking, fixing, or hiding the perceived defects (Phillips, 2014; Barnhill et al., 2023). These compulsions, which can be behavioral (e.g., mirror checking, excessive grooming, skin picking) or mental acts (e.g., comparing one's appearance to others), are difficult to control and contribute substantially to the disorder's morbidity (Phillips, 2014; Phillips & Stein, 2015).

From a biological and neurological perspective, BDD represents a fascinating and complex puzzle at the intersection of perception, cognition, emotion, and behavior (Szabó, 2010). While historically viewed through a purely psychological lens, a growing body of evidence points toward a significant neurobiological substrate underlying its perplexing symptoms (Winter & Buhlmann, 2013; Szabó & Szászi, 2021). The classification of BDD within the obsessive-compulsive and related disorders (OCRD) category in the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5) underscores its phenomenological and pathophysiological overlap with Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD) (Barnhill et al., 2023; Löchner et al., 2017; Phillips & Stein, 2015). This link provides a crucial framework for exploring the neurobiological underpinnings of BDD, particularly concerning neurotransmitter systems and neural circuitry (Neziroglu & Lauren, 2012; Phillips, 1998).

The profound distortion of body image, a central feature of the disorder (Veale, 2004; Hosseini & Padhy, 2019), suggests dysfunction in the brain's complex systems for constructing a coherent and stable representation of the self (Morin, 2009; Ho & Lenggenhager, 2021). This is not merely a matter of low self-esteem but appears to involve fundamental anomalies in visual and sensory processing, selective attention, and self-referential thought (Rasmussen et al., 2013; Buchanan et al., 2011). The fact that individuals with BDD often hold their beliefs with poor or absent insight, sometimes reaching delusional intensity (Phillips, 2014; Barnhill et al., 2023), further points to disruptions in higher-order cognitive and executive functions responsible for reality testing and belief flexibility (Konstantakopoulos, 2019).

This chapter aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the biological and neurological basis of BDD, synthesizing evidence from neuropharmacology, neuroimaging, genetics, and cognitive neuroscience. By examining the intricate interplay of these biological factors, we can move toward a more integrated biopsychosocial understanding of BDD's etiology and maintenance (Winter & Buhlmann, 2013; Szabó, 2010). This deeper understanding is not only of academic interest but is essential for refining diagnostic approaches, developing more targeted and effective treatments, and ultimately alleviating the immense suffering associated with this hidden and severe disorder (Phillips, 2014; Jefferys & Castle, 2003).

## Neurobiological Systems

The neurobiological investigation of Body Dysmorphic Disorder, while still in its relative infancy compared to other major psychiatric illnesses, has begun to illuminate the complex interplay of neurotransmitter systems and neural circuits that contribute to its core symptoms of perceptual distortion, obsessional thinking, and compulsive behaviors (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020). The conceptualization of BDD as an obsessive-compulsive related disorder provides a critical foundation for this exploration, suggesting shared pathophysiological mechanisms with OCD (Löchner et al., 2017; Phillips, 1998).

### Neurotransmitter Systems and Neuropharmacology

The most compelling evidence for neurochemical dysfunction in BDD comes from psychopharmacological treatment studies (Phillips, 2009). The efficacy of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) as a first-line treatment for BDD strongly implicates the serotonin (5-HT) system in its pathophysiology (Slaughter & Sun, 1999; Veale, 2004; Phillips, 1998). SSRIs are often effective even when BDD symptoms are held with delusional conviction, suggesting that the serotonergic dysfunction is central to the disorder's mechanism rather than being secondary to a particular level of insight (Phillips, 1998). The clinical observation that higher doses of SSRIs are often necessary for BDD, similar to what is seen in OCD, further strengthens the hypothesis of a shared serotonergic deficit between these two disorders (Veale, 2004; Slaughter & Sun, 1999). The therapeutic action of SRIs in BDD (Shiffman, 2008) suggests that the core features of obsessional preoccupation and compulsive behaviors are modulated, at least in part, by serotonergic pathways (Phillips, 2009). The serotonin system is deeply involved in regulating mood, anxiety, and impulse control (Sarkar, 2016), all of which are domains significantly affected in BDD (Löchner et al., 2017). Dysfunction in this system could contribute to the persistent, intrusive nature of appearance-related thoughts (obsessions) and the inability to inhibit repetitive checking and grooming behaviors (compulsions) (Clark & Purdon, 2003; Pallanti et al., 2011).

The role of other neurotransmitter systems is less clear but warrants consideration, particularly the dopamine system. Dopamine pathways are crucial for reward, motivation, and belief formation (Sussman, 2025). The poor or absent insight characteristic of BDD, which can reach delusional proportions, may suggest dopaminergic dysregulation (Barnhill et al., 2023; Phillips, 2014). Delusional disorders, particularly the somatic type which shares features with BDD, have been linked to dopamine hyperactivity (Manschreck, 1996; Ajiboye & Yusuf, 2013). However, the general lack of efficacy of antipsychotic drugs (dopamine antagonists) when used as monotherapy in BDD suggests that dopamine dysregulation is not the primary driver of the disorder, unlike in primary psychotic disorders such as schizophrenia (Veale, 2004; Rabe‐Jabłońska, 1998). Nonetheless, some evidence suggests that antipsychotics may be useful as an augmenting agent to SSRIs, particularly in patients with delusional beliefs (Phillips, 1998), hinting at a potential modulatory role for dopamine in the context of underlying serotonergic dysfunction.

The noradrenergic system, heavily involved in the body's stress and arousal responses (the "fight or flight" reaction), is also likely implicated (Wiegant, 1991; Chu et al., 2019). The high rates of comorbid anxiety disorders, such as social anxiety disorder, and the prominent anxiety symptoms experienced by individuals with BDD point to a state of chronic hyperarousal (Hollander & Aronowitz, 1999; Manivannan, 2018). This hyperarousal may manifest as hypervigilance towards perceived flaws and an exaggerated emotional response to social situations where they fear scrutiny (Saeed, 2009; Bebchuk & Tancer, 1999). Some antidepressants that modulate norepinephrine, such as serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), have shown efficacy in treating anxiety and depressive symptoms, which are often comorbid with or secondary to BDD (McElroy et al., 2006; Brandish & Baldwin, 2012). This suggests that noradrenergic dysregulation may contribute significantly to the affective distress and functional impairment seen in BDD, even if it is not the primary driver of the core perceptual and obsessional symptoms.

### Neural Circuits and Functional Connectivity

Converging evidence from neuroimaging and neuropsychological studies suggests that BDD arises from dysfunction within specific large-scale neural circuits, particularly those connecting frontal, limbic, and visual processing areas (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020). These circuits are fundamental for integrating sensory information, regulating emotional responses, and exerting cognitive control over thoughts and behaviors.

**Fronto-striatal Circuits:** The cortico-striatal-thalamo-cortical (CSTC) loops are strongly implicated in both OCD and BDD (Huang, 2012; Buchanan et al., 2011). These circuits, which involve connections between the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), and basal ganglia (particularly the caudate nucleus and putamen), are critical for behavioral inhibition, habit formation, and emotional processing (Zermatten, 2007). In OCD, hyperactivity in these loops is thought to underlie the inability to suppress intrusive thoughts and inhibit compulsive behaviors (Yager, 2015; Pallanti et al., 2011). Functional neuroimaging studies have identified abnormalities in the caudate nucleus in individuals with BDD, a key structure within the striatum (Spence, 2006). This finding provides a direct neuroanatomical link to the pathophysiology of OCD and suggests that the repetitive, ritualistic behaviors in BDD (e.g., mirror checking, skin picking) may be driven by similar fronto-striatal dysregulation (Phillips & Stein, 2015; Hand, 1995). Dysfunction in this circuitry could explain the failure of behavioral inhibition that allows compulsions to become entrenched and difficult to resist (Matsunaga, 2012).

**Limbic System and Emotional Processing:** The limbic system, particularly the amygdala, is central to the processing of emotions, especially fear and threat (Kiecolt‐Glaser et al., 2020). Neurobiological models of BDD propose that hyperactivity in the amygdala may contribute to the intense anxiety, fear, and emotional distress associated with perceived appearance flaws (Buchanan et al., 2011). This emotional hyperarousal could amplify the negative perception of one's appearance, reinforcing the belief that the "flaw" is dangerous or socially unacceptable (Buchanan et al., 2011). Neuroimaging studies have indeed found abnormalities in the right amygdala in BDD patients, supporting its role in the emotional hyperreactivity characteristic of the disorder (Buchanan et al., 2011). This limbic hyperactivity might also explain the misinterpretation of neutral or ambiguous social cues as negative or judgmental, a common experience for those with BDD that fuels social anxiety and avoidance (Hollander & Aronowitz, 1999). The high comorbidity with anxiety and depressive disorders further supports the involvement of dysregulated limbic circuits in BDD (Sarkar, 2016; Phillips, 2009).

**Visual and Perceptual Processing Networks:** A defining feature of BDD is a profound distortion of visual perception and body image (Veale, 2004; Hosseini & Padhy, 2019). This suggests abnormalities in the brain's visual processing networks. Research indicates that individuals with BDD may have a cognitive bias towards processing detailed, local visual information at the expense of holistic, global processing (Buchanan et al., 2011; Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017). This analytical processing style could lead individuals to focus excessively on minor imperfections (e.g., a single pore or a slight asymmetry) while failing to perceive their overall normal appearance. Neuroimaging studies have found structural and functional abnormalities in the visual cortex of BDD patients, providing a neural correlate for this biased processing style (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020). Furthermore, posterior parietal and premotor areas are crucial for constructing the "body schema," the brain's dynamic representation of the body in space, while cortical midline structures are involved in the "body image," the cognitive and affective representation of one's appearance (Ho & Lenggenhager, 2021). Dysfunction in the connectivity between these visual, parietal, and self-referential processing regions could underlie the fundamental misperception of the body that defines BDD (Spence, 2006; Ho & Lenggenhager, 2021). The experience of a disturbed body image is not unique to BDD, and can be seen after focal brain lesions, particularly of the right hemisphere, further implicating these networks (Morin, 2009; Stunkard & Mendelson, 1967).

## Genetic and Molecular Basis

While environmental and psychological factors are undoubtedly important in the development of BDD (Winter & Buhlmann, 2013), evidence suggests a significant contribution from genetic and molecular mechanisms. The clustering of BDD within the obsessive-compulsive and related disorders (OCRDs) provides a valuable framework for investigating its genetic architecture, as these disorders are known to have a substantial heritable component (Barnhill et al., 2023; Phillips & Stein, 2015). Although research specifically on the genetics of BDD is limited within the provided sources, we can infer potential pathways by examining related disorders and the broader principles of psychiatric genetics.

### Heritability and Family Studies

The observation that psychiatric disorders often run in families suggests a genetic predisposition (Rice et al., January 2002; Hechtman, 1991). While no specific family studies of BDD are detailed in the provided literature, the strong clinical and phenomenological overlap with OCD allows for reasonable inference (Löchner et al., 2017; Neziroglu & Lauren, 2012). OCD itself has a recognized familial tendency (King & Nepps, 1993), suggesting that BDD may share some of this genetic vulnerability. One study noted that 5.8% of patients with BDD had a first-degree relative with the same pathology, hinting at familial aggregation (Campuzano Cordero, 2016). The broader concept of a predisposition to mental illness, involving a combination of genetic and environmental influences, is a well-established principle in psychiatry (Alderton, 1966; Scheid & Wright, 2017). Therefore, it is highly plausible that genetic factors contribute to an individual's risk of developing BDD, likely by influencing personality traits, neurodevelopmental pathways, or neurotransmitter system function (Szabó, 2010; Szabó & Szászi, 2021). Traits such as neuroticism, which is a disposition to experience negative affects like anxiety and self-consciousness, have a strong heritable component and are highly associated with a wide array of psychopathologies, including anxiety and mood disorders that are frequently comorbid with BDD (Widiger & Oltmanns, 2017; Phillips, 2009).

### Candidate Genes and Molecular Mechanisms

The primary molecular mechanism implicated in BDD is the serotonergic system, based on the established efficacy of SSRIs (Veale, 2004; Phillips, 1998; Slaughter & Sun, 1999). This suggests that genes involved in serotonin synthesis, transport, and receptor function could be important candidates for BDD vulnerability. For instance, polymorphisms in the serotonin transporter gene (SLC6A4) have been extensively studied in relation to depression, anxiety, and OCD (Dedic et al., 2011). Variations in this gene could influence the efficiency of serotonin reuptake at the synapse, altering synaptic serotonin levels and contributing to the dysregulation seen in BDD. While direct evidence from the provided papers is absent, this represents a logical avenue for future genetic research based on the pharmacological data.

Similarly, given the potential role of dopamine in delusionality and reward processing (Sussman, 2025), genes related to the dopamine system, such as those for dopamine receptors (e.g., DRD2, DRD3) or the dopamine transporter (DAT1), could be relevant, particularly for BDD with absent insight/delusional beliefs (Barnhill et al., 2023). Studies in schizophrenia have long focused on the dopamine hypothesis (Buckley & Foster, 2014), and while BDD is distinct, the shared feature of delusional thinking in a subset of patients suggests a potential, albeit likely complex, overlap in molecular pathways (Giotakos, 2017).

Beyond monoamines, neurodevelopmental genes are another area of interest. BDD typically begins in adolescence (Albertini & Phillips, 2002; Grau et al., 2014), a critical period of brain development and synaptic pruning, particularly in the prefrontal cortex. Genes that guide these neurodevelopmental processes could contribute to vulnerability. For example, disorders like autism spectrum disorder (ASD), which are defined by their neurodevelopmental origins, are characterized by impairments in social communication and repetitive behaviors (Reeve & King, 2018; Wolf & Paterson, 2010). The occasional comorbidity of BDD with ASD, and the shared features of repetitive behaviors and atypical sensory processing, suggest that disruptions in genes governing neural connectivity and development could be a shared risk factor (Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017).

Furthermore, the significant role of stress in triggering and exacerbating psychiatric disorders points to the importance of genes involved in the stress response system (Kapfhammer, 2018; Wiegant, 1991). The hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis is the body's primary stress response system, and its function is regulated by numerous genes (Chu et al., 2019). Chronic stress can lead to dysregulation of the HPA axis, resulting in altered cortisol levels, which have been implicated in depression and anxiety disorders (Meyer & Hamel, 2014; Pervanidou & Chrousos, 2011). Genes that regulate HPA axis function, such as those for the glucocorticoid receptor, could influence an individual's resilience or vulnerability to developing BDD following stressful life events or childhood adversity (van der Kolk, 1998), which are noted risk factors for the disorder (Veale, 2004).

## Brain Structure and Function

The study of brain structure and function in Body Dysmorphic Disorder provides a critical bridge between the subjective experience of the patient and the underlying neurobiological mechanisms. Through neuroimaging and neuropsychological assessment, researchers have begun to map the neural correlates of BDD's core symptoms, including perceptual distortions, obsessive thoughts, and emotional dysregulation (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020). These findings largely align with the circuit-based models discussed previously, highlighting dysfunction in visual, emotional, and executive control networks.

### Neuroimaging Findings: Structural and Functional

Although the literature provided is not exhaustive in primary neuroimaging studies, it contains key reviews and reports that point to consistent patterns of brain abnormalities in BDD. Functional neuroimaging, which measures brain activity, and structural neuroimaging, which assesses brain anatomy, have both contributed to our understanding.

**Structural Findings:** Structural magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) studies have identified abnormalities in several brain regions. One of the most consistent areas of focus is the prefrontal cortex (PFC), a region critical for executive functions like planning, decision-making, and impulse control (Wheeler, 2014; Golden, 2015). Abnormalities in the PFC, particularly the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), are thought to contribute to the poor insight and impaired judgment seen in BDD (Buchanan et al., 2011). The OFC is part of a larger fronto-striatal circuit, and structural changes here could disrupt the top-down cognitive control necessary to inhibit intrusive thoughts and compulsive behaviors (Huang, 2012). The finding of structural brain damage in the frontal cortex being associated with delusional infestation, a related somatic delusional disorder, lends further support to the role of this region in maintaining reality-based beliefs about the body (Lepping et al., 2015). Additionally, some studies have noted alterations in the visual cortex, which could be a direct anatomical correlate of the biased visual processing observed in the disorder (Buchanan et al., 2011).

**Functional Findings:** Functional neuroimaging studies, such as functional MRI (fMRI) and single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT), have provided dynamic insights into brain activity in BDD. These studies often reveal hyperactivity in brain regions associated with emotional processing and threat detection, particularly the amygdala (Buchanan et al., 2011). This amygdalar hyperarousal is thought to underlie the intense fear, anxiety, and distress that individuals with BDD experience in response to their perceived flaws (Buchanan et al., 2011). This finding is consistent with the broader neurobiology of anxiety disorders, where amygdala hyperactivity is a common feature (Baldwin et al., 2008).

Furthermore, functional studies corroborate the structural findings of fronto-striatal circuit dysfunction. There is evidence of abnormal activity in the orbitofrontal cortex and the caudate nucleus, key nodes of the CSTC loops (Spence, 2006; Buchanan et al., 2011). This aberrant activity pattern is believed to drive the obsessive preoccupations and compulsive rituals that characterize BDD, mirroring findings in OCD (Yager, 2015; Pallanti et al., 2011). SPECT imaging in a case of dissociative amnesia, a disorder involving trauma and altered self-perception, revealed decreased perfusion in the left medial temporal lobe and basal forebrain, suggesting that dysfunction in memory and self-related circuits can manifest as profound psychological symptoms (Kurita et al., 2004). Given the role of past experiences and self-focus in BDD, similar network disruptions may be at play.

The brain's processing of visual information is also demonstrably different in BDD. When viewing faces (including their own), individuals with BDD show abnormal activation patterns in the visual cortex and related areas (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020). This may reflect the detail-oriented, analytical processing style that leads to a hyper-focus on minor imperfections (Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017). The brain may be failing to integrate these details into a holistic and accurate perception of the face or body part, a process dependent on coordinated activity between primary visual areas and higher-order association cortices in the parietal and temporal lobes (Ho & Lenggenhager, 2021; Morin, 2009).

### Neuropsychological and Cognitive Deficits

Neuropsychological testing provides behavioral evidence of the cognitive deficits that arise from underlying brain dysfunction. In BDD, these deficits are not typically global but are concentrated in specific domains that align with the neuroimaging findings.

**Executive Functions:** Patients with BDD often exhibit subtle deficits in executive functions (Buchanan et al., 2011). These functions, largely subserved by the prefrontal cortex, include cognitive flexibility, planning, and inhibition (Godefroy & Stuss, 2007). Deficits in cognitive flexibility may manifest as an inability to shift attention away from perceived flaws or to consider alternative, less negative interpretations of their appearance. Impaired inhibition is consistent with the difficulty patients experience in resisting compulsive behaviors like mirror checking or skin picking (Phillips & Stein, 2015). The poor insight seen in BDD can also be conceptualized as a deficit in self-monitoring and cognitive self-reflection, which are core executive abilities (Konstantakopoulos, 2019; Harvey & Pinkham, 2015).

**Visual Processing and Memory:** As mentioned, a key neuropsychological finding in BDD is a bias toward local, detail-oriented visual processing over global, holistic processing (Buchanan et al., 2011; Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017). This is often demonstrated on tasks that require individuals to identify larger figures composed of smaller elements. Individuals with BDD may be quicker to identify the local elements but slower to perceive the global figure. This cognitive style is thought to contribute directly to the clinical picture, where patients become fixated on minute details of their appearance (Rasmussen et al., 2013). There is also evidence of a memory bias for detailed visual information in BDD (Buchanan et al., 2011). This may mean that individuals with the disorder are more likely to encode and recall specific, often negatively interpreted, details about their appearance, reinforcing their distorted body image over time. This is consistent with findings in other disorders, where cognitive biases in attention and memory play a crucial role in maintaining psychopathology (Unknown, 2018).

**Social Cognition:** Social cognition refers to the mental operations that underlie social interactions, including the ability to perceive and interpret the emotions and intentions of others (Alberdi-Páramo & Pelaz-Antolín, 2019). Individuals with BDD often misinterpret others' facial expressions or comments as being negative and directed at their appearance (Phillips, 2014). This is supported by findings of emotional hyperactivity and misinterpretation of emotion in others (Buchanan et al., 2011). Such deficits in social cognition are likely linked to the amygdala hyperreactivity discussed earlier and contribute significantly to the profound social anxiety and avoidance that characterize the disorder (Hollander & Aronowitz, 1999; Schneier et al., 2014).

## Developmental Neurobiology

Body Dysmorphic Disorder typically emerges during adolescence, a critical period of neurobiological and psychosocial development (Albertini & Phillips, 2002; Grau et al., 2014; Szabó, 2010). This specific window of onset suggests that the disorder's origins may be deeply rooted in the complex maturational processes of the brain, interacting with the heightened social and self-evaluative pressures of this life stage (Zauner, 1978). Understanding the developmental neurobiology of BDD requires examining how normative brain development may go awry, leading to the characteristic symptoms of the disorder.

### Onset and Critical Periods

The average age of onset for BDD is during the teenage years (Albertini & Phillips, 2002; Nakao et al., 2017). Adolescence is marked by significant physical changes (e.g., puberty) and a heightened focus on appearance and peer acceptance (Hosseini & Padhy, 2019; Muuss, 1986). These normative developmental challenges occur concurrently with substantial reorganization of key neural circuits. The prefrontal cortex (PFC), which is responsible for executive functions like impulse control, self-monitoring, and emotional regulation, undergoes protracted development that continues into early adulthood (Wheeler, 2014). The limbic system, including the amygdala, which governs emotional reactivity, also matures during this time, often leading to heightened emotional sensitivity (Casey & Bailey, 2011).

This temporal overlap is highly significant. A vulnerability in the development of PFC-limbic connectivity could lead to a failure of top-down cognitive control over emotional responses (Sandi, 2013). In individuals who develop BDD, normative adolescent concerns about appearance might be amplified by a hyperreactive amygdala, while an underdeveloped or inefficient PFC fails to regulate the resulting anxiety and distress (Buchanan et al., 2011). This could establish a vicious cycle where negative self-perceptions trigger intense emotional reactions that, in turn, are not adequately modulated by cognitive control, leading to the entrenchment of obsessive thoughts and compulsive behaviors (Rasmussen et al., 2013). The onset of other related disorders, such as OCD, social anxiety disorder, and eating disorders, also frequently occurs during adolescence, suggesting a shared period of neurodevelopmental vulnerability for this spectrum of conditions (Ross & Gowers, 2011; Hechtman, 1991; Harper, 1994).

### Role of Early Life Experiences and Trauma

Early life experiences, including trauma, abuse, and neglect, are recognized as potent risk factors for a wide range of psychiatric disorders (van der Kolk, 1998; Meyer & Hamel, 2014). These experiences can have lasting effects on neurodevelopment, particularly on the stress response systems and emotional regulation circuits (Copeland‐Linder et al., 2017). Childhood maltreatment, for example, is associated with an exaggerated response to stress later in life (Schulman, 2011). Studies have noted that bullying, abuse, or other adverse life events during childhood or adolescence may be associated with the development of BDD (Veale, 2004). Such traumatic events can profoundly disrupt the development of a stable sense of self and body image (Morin, 2009; Krueger, 2001).

From a neurobiological standpoint, chronic stress during development can alter the structure and function of the hippocampus and amygdala, brain regions critical for memory and emotion (Sandi, 2013; Kapfhammer, 2018). It can also sensitize the HPA axis, leading to a state of chronic hyperarousal (Chu et al., 2019). For a developing adolescent, a history of being teased about their appearance (Hosseini & Padhy, 2019) could interact with this neurobiological vulnerability, leading to the misinterpretation of benign physical features as defective and catastrophic. The content of BDD preoccupations may be symbolically linked to these early experiences (Sündermann & Veale, 2017). For example, a person who experienced emotional neglect might develop a preoccupation with "empty" or "dull" looking skin, a physical manifestation of their internal state (Charmaz, 1983). Dissociative phenomena, which are common responses to trauma, involve a disruption in the normal integration of consciousness, memory, and identity (Kihlstrom, 2004; Cardeña, 2008). The profound disconnect from one's own body seen in BDD shares features with dissociative experiences like depersonalization, where individuals feel detached from their own body or mental processes (Michal, 2022; Simeon & Abugel, 2006). This suggests that some BDD symptoms may represent a form of trauma-related dissociation focused on the body (Sündermann & Veale, 2017; Tobin et al., 1995).

### Neurodevelopmental Trajectories and Heterogeneity

The developmental pathways leading to BDD are likely heterogeneous. Some individuals may have a primary neurodevelopmental vulnerability, perhaps genetic, that affects visual processing or executive function from an early age (Alderton, 1966). For these individuals, BDD symptoms might emerge more insidiously as social and cognitive demands increase during adolescence. This pathway may be more common in individuals with comorbid neurodevelopmental disorders like ASD or ADHD (Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017; Anastopoulos et al., 2003). For example, the tendency for local, detail-focused processing seen in ASD could predispose an individual to fixate on minor physical flaws (Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017).

In other individuals, BDD may develop more acutely following specific psychosocial stressors or traumatic events in a person with a pre-existing temperamental vulnerability, such as high neuroticism or perfectionism (Widiger & Oltmanns, 2017; Steinert et al., 2021). This pathway might be more associated with high rates of comorbid depression and anxiety (Manivannan, 2018; Hollander & Aronowitz, 1999). The interaction between an individual's innate temperament and their early environment shapes the development of emotion regulation and coping strategies (Jenkins & Oatley, 1998). A failure to develop adaptive coping skills during childhood may leave an adolescent ill-equipped to handle the normative stresses of puberty and social evaluation, increasing the risk for developing a maladaptive coping strategy like the obsessions and compulsions of BDD (Vogel, 1985). The timing of the onset of BDD symptoms, whether insidious or acute, and the presence of specific comorbidities may thus reflect different underlying developmental neurobiological trajectories that warrant further investigation.

## Treatment Mechanisms

Understanding the biological basis of treatments for Body Dysmorphic Disorder is crucial for optimizing clinical care and developing novel interventions. The primary evidence-based treatments for BDD, pharmacotherapy with SSRIs and cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), are believed to exert their effects by targeting the specific neurobiological dysfunctions identified in the disorder (Phillips, 1998; Rasmussen et al., 2013).

### Neuroplasticity and Pharmacological Interventions

The efficacy of SSRIs provides the strongest evidence for the biological basis of BDD treatment (Jefferys & Castle, 2003; Hunt et al., 2008; Veale, 2004). SSRIs work by blocking the reuptake of serotonin in the synaptic cleft, thereby increasing the availability of this neurotransmitter to postsynaptic receptors (Slaughter & Sun, 1999). This action is thought to correct an underlying serotonergic hypofunction (Slaughter & Sun, 1999). Over time, this sustained increase in synaptic serotonin leads to a cascade of downstream effects, including changes in receptor density and sensitivity, and alterations in gene expression, which collectively contribute to neuroplastic changes in the brain circuits implicated in BDD (Dedic et al., 2011).

Specifically, the therapeutic effects of SSRIs in BDD are likely mediated by their impact on the CSTC and limbic circuits (Phillips, 2009). By modulating serotonergic tone within these pathways, SSRIs may help to dampen the hyperactivity of the orbitofrontal cortex and amygdala (Buchanan et al., 2011). This could lead to a reduction in the intensity and frequency of intrusive, obsessive thoughts about appearance and a decrease in the associated anxiety and emotional distress (Phillips, 1998). Furthermore, by enhancing top-down control from the prefrontal cortex, SSRIs may improve behavioral inhibition, making it easier for patients to resist compulsive behaviors like mirror checking and skin picking (Pallanti et al., 2011). The fact that higher doses and longer treatment durations are often required in BDD suggests that significant neuroadaptive changes are necessary to achieve clinical improvement (Slaughter & Sun, 1999; Hill, 2021).

In cases with poor insight or delusional beliefs, augmentation with atypical antipsychotics may be considered (Phillips, 1998). These medications primarily block dopamine D2 receptors, but also have effects on serotonin and other neurotransmitter systems (Montes & Rey, 2009). Their potential benefit in BDD may stem from modulating dopaminergic pathways involved in belief formation and salience attribution, thereby reducing the conviction with which patients hold their appearance-related beliefs (Giotakos, 2017). This suggests a synergistic effect, where SSRIs address the core obsessive-compulsive-anxious components, and antipsychotics target the delusional/overvalued ideation component.

### Compensatory Mechanisms and Psychotherapy

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), particularly with exposure and response prevention (ERP), is the most empirically supported psychosocial intervention for BDD (Rasmussen et al., 2013; Sündermann & Veale, 2017). From a neurobiological perspective, CBT can be understood as a "top-down" intervention that induces neuroplastic changes through targeted learning and behavioral practice (Rasmussen et al., 2013).

The "cognitive restructuring" component of CBT targets the maladaptive thoughts and beliefs related to appearance (e.g., "Everyone is looking at my nose") (Rasmussen et al., 2013). By teaching patients to identify, challenge, and reframe these distorted cognitions, CBT may strengthen prefrontal cortical control over limbic structures (Sandi, 2013). This process is a form of neuroplasticity, where repeated practice of new ways of thinking can create and reinforce new, healthier neural pathways, reducing the automatic activation of negative, appearance-focused schemas.

Exposure and response prevention (ERP) is the behavioral core of CBT for BDD (Rasmussen et al., 2013). ERP involves gradually and systematically confronting feared or avoided situations (e.g., going out in public without camouflage) while refraining from engaging in compulsive rituals (e.g., mirror checking) (Rasmussen et al., 2013). This process directly targets the neural circuits of fear and habit. Through exposure, the patient learns through experience that the feared consequences do not occur, leading to habituation and extinction of the fear response, a process mediated by the amygdala and ventromedial PFC (Hoon, 2012). Response prevention works to break the link between the obsessive thought/anxiety and the compulsive behavior, weakening the maladaptive habit pathways in the fronto-striatal circuits (Yager, 2015). Over time, ERP can lead to a "rewiring" of these circuits, making the compulsive behaviors less automatic and easier to resist.

Other techniques integrated into CBT for BDD, such as perceptual retraining and mindfulness, also have neurobiological implications (Rasmussen et al., 2013). Perceptual retraining exercises, which encourage patients to describe their appearance in a more objective, non-evaluative way and to focus on their appearance as a whole rather than on isolated details, may help to correct the biased, detail-oriented visual processing style seen in BDD (Buchanan et al., 2011). This may promote more holistic visual processing, engaging broader visual association cortices and reducing the hyper-focus driven by localized visual cortex activity. Mindfulness techniques, which involve paying attention to the present moment non-judgmentally, can improve emotional regulation and reduce rumination, potentially by strengthening PFC-mediated control over the amygdala and default mode network activity (Barnhofer and Fennell, 2008).

It is important to note that cosmetic procedures are generally ineffective and often worsen BDD symptoms (Phillips, 2014; Crerand et al., 2010; Kuhn et al., 2018). From a neurobiological standpoint, this is because such procedures fail to address the underlying brain-based disorder of perception and belief (Brito et al., 2016). The "defect" is not in the skin, but in the brain's interpretation of it; therefore, altering the skin does not correct the core pathology and the preoccupation often shifts to a new body part (Kuhn et al., 2018).

## Future Directions

While our understanding of the biological and neurological basis of Body Dysmorphic Disorder has advanced significantly, the field is still replete with unanswered questions and promising avenues for future research. The current knowledge base, largely built on inferences from OCD and other related disorders, highlights the urgent need for studies specifically focused on the unique neurobiology of BDD (Phillips, 2014). Future investigations should aim to move beyond broad associations and delineate the specific mechanisms that drive the disorder's distinct phenomenology.

One of the most critical areas for future research is genetics. Large-scale genome-wide association studies (GWAS) specifically in BDD cohorts are needed to identify common genetic variants that confer risk for the disorder. Such studies could illuminate novel biological pathways and help to disentangle the genetic overlap with OCD, social anxiety disorder, and major depression (Löchner et al., 2017; Hollander & Aronowitz, 1999). Investigating the role of rare genetic variants and epigenetic modifications, such as DNA methylation, could also provide insights into how environmental factors like early trauma or stress interact with genetic predisposition to shape neurodevelopment and increase BDD risk (Meyer & Hamel, 2014; Kapfhammer, 2018).

In the realm of neuroimaging, future studies should employ more sophisticated, multimodal approaches. Combining structural MRI, functional MRI (fMRI), and techniques like diffusion tensor imaging (DTI) can provide a more comprehensive picture of both the anatomy and connectivity of the brain circuits implicated in BDD (Maeshima et al., 2012). Longitudinal imaging studies are particularly needed to track brain changes from adolescence—the typical period of onset—through adulthood (Albertini & Phillips, 2002). This would allow researchers to determine whether observed brain abnormalities are a cause or a consequence of the illness and to identify neurodevelopmental markers that predict the onset of BDD. Task-based fMRI studies could be designed to probe specific cognitive and perceptual processes, such as holistic versus detail-oriented visual processing of faces and bodies, or the neural response to perceived social scrutiny, linking behavioral deficits directly to circuit-level dysfunction (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020).

The exploration of novel neurobiological systems beyond the classic monoamines is another important frontier. For example, the glutamatergic system, which is the primary excitatory system in the brain, has been implicated in the pathophysiology of OCD and may play a role in BDD, particularly concerning cognitive rigidity and compulsive behaviors (Karakatsoulis et al., 2021). Similarly, the role of the brain's immune system and neuroinflammatory processes deserves investigation, given the links between inflammation, stress, and mood and anxiety disorders (Die Trill, 2012; Kiecolt‐Glaser et al., 2020). Investigating biomarkers such as pro-inflammatory cytokines could provide new diagnostic tools and therapeutic targets (Bhardwaj et al., 2024).

Developing and validating animal models that capture key features of BDD, such as repetitive grooming or social avoidance in response to a perceived physical anomaly, could be invaluable (Dodman & Olivier, 1996). While challenging, such models would allow for more invasive and controlled investigation of neural circuits and molecular pathways, and could serve as a platform for screening novel pharmacological agents (Dedic et al., 2011).

From a clinical and treatment perspective, future research should focus on identifying biological subtypes of BDD. It is likely that BDD is not a monolithic entity; subtypes based on insight level (non-delusional vs. delusional), primary preoccupation (e.g., skin, hair, muscularity), or comorbidity patterns may have distinct neurobiological signatures (Sreshta et al., 2017; Barnhill et al., 2023). Identifying these subtypes could lead to more personalized and effective treatments (Veale et al., 2017). For example, patients with delusional BDD might benefit more from treatments that target dopaminergic systems, while those with prominent anxiety might respond to noradrenergic agents. Neuroimaging could also be used to predict treatment response, identifying which patients are most likely to benefit from SSRIs versus CBT, or a combination of both (Summers et al., 2023).

Finally, the neurobiological mechanisms of successful treatment need to be better understood. Longitudinal studies that conduct neuroimaging or other biological assessments before and after a course of SSRI treatment or CBT could reveal the specific neuroplastic changes that underlie clinical improvement. Understanding how these treatments "normalize" brain function could lead to the refinement of existing therapies and the development of novel, brain-based interventions, such as non-invasive brain stimulation techniques (e.g., transcranial magnetic stimulation) aimed at directly modulating the activity of dysfunctional circuits. As our knowledge of the biological underpinnings of BDD grows, so too will our capacity to offer more precise, effective, and lasting relief to those who suffer from it.

## Conclusion

Body Dysmorphic Disorder is a severe psychiatric condition rooted in a complex interplay of biological, neurological, and psychological factors (Winter & Buhlmann, 2013; Szabó, 2010). This chapter has synthesized the available evidence to construct a neurobiological model of the disorder, moving beyond a purely psychological explanation to one that integrates genetics, neurotransmitter systems, neural circuitry, and cognitive neuroscience. The evidence strongly suggests that BDD is a brain-based disorder, characterized by specific and identifiable dysfunctions in neural systems responsible for perception, emotion regulation, and cognitive control (Buchanan et al., 2011; Spence, 2006).

The pronounced efficacy of serotonin reuptake inhibitors points to a central role for the serotonergic system in the pathophysiology of BDD, linking it closely with obsessive-compulsive disorder (Veale, 2004; Phillips, 1998; Slaughter & Sun, 1999). This neurochemical imbalance appears to manifest at the circuit level, with converging evidence implicating dysregulation within fronto-striatal loops, which drive the disorder's obsessive-compulsive features, and hyperactivity in limbic structures like the amygdala, which fuels the intense anxiety and emotional distress (Buchanan et al., 2011; Rincón Barreto et al., 2020).

Crucially, the defining symptom of BDD—a distorted body image—appears to have a distinct neural correlate. Abnormalities in visual processing networks, coupled with a cognitive bias toward detail-oriented analysis, provide a compelling explanation for how individuals can become fixated on minute, non-existent, or slight flaws while failing to perceive their overall appearance accurately (Rasmussen et al., 2013; Vasudeva & Hollander, 2017). Dysfunction in higher-order brain regions responsible for integrating sensory input into a coherent body schema and image further solidifies the neurological basis of this profound perceptual disturbance (Ho & Lenggenhager, 2021).

The typical onset of BDD during adolescence underscores the importance of a neurodevelopmental perspective, suggesting that the disorder may arise from an interaction between genetic vulnerability and environmental stressors during a critical period of brain maturation (Albertini & Phillips, 2002; Veale, 2004). This framework helps explain the heterogeneity of the disorder and its high rates of comorbidity with other conditions that share similar developmental and neurobiological risk factors.

Ultimately, viewing BDD through a neurobiological lens has profound clinical implications. It validates the immense suffering of patients, framing their experience not as vanity or a character flaw, but as the manifestation of a legitimate brain disorder. It provides a clear rationale for the use of evidence-based treatments like SSRIs and CBT, which are understood to work by inducing neuroplastic changes that normalize brain circuit function (Phillips, 2009; Rasmussen et al., 2013). While many questions remain, the continued investigation into the biological and neurological underpinnings of BDD holds the promise of more precise diagnostics, novel therapeutic targets, and ultimately, a greater capacity to restore health and quality of life for those affected by this challenging and often hidden illness (Phillips, 2014).
